# Medical-Only Immunization Exemption Standards Act

## Section 1: Purpose and Findings

(a) The Legislature finds and declares the following:

(1) Childhood immunization is one of the most effective public health interventions in modern medicine, preventing millions of cases of serious illness and death annually.

(2) States that limit immunization exemptions to medical-only consistently achieve the highest vaccination rates in the nation. West Virginia maintained a 98.3% MMR vaccination rate among kindergarteners, ranking first in the nation, with a non-medical exemption rate of less than 0.1%. Mississippi sustained vaccination rates above 97% for decades under its medical-only exemption standard, ranking among the top states nationally until it was compelled to allow religious exemptions in 2023, after which its ranking fell from first to third.

(3) In 2015, following a measles outbreak linked to Disneyland in Anaheim, California, the California Legislature enacted SB 277, eliminating personal belief exemptions from school immunization requirements. Vaccination rates increased measurably in the years following enactment.

(4) Currently, only five states — West Virginia, California, New York, Connecticut, and Maine — maintain medical-only exemption policies for school and childcare entry.

(5) Peer-reviewed research demonstrates a strong correlation between the availability of non-medical exemptions and the clustering of unvaccinated individuals in communities, which in turn is associated with outbreaks of vaccine-preventable diseases including measles, whooping cough, and other communicable illnesses.

(6) It is the purpose of this Act to protect children and the public health by ensuring that only medically justified exemptions are permitted from school and childcare immunization requirements.

## Section 2: Definitions

As used in this Act:

(a) **"Immunization requirements"** means the vaccinations required by the State Department of Health for enrollment in any school or childcare facility, as established by rule in accordance with recommendations of the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) and the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP).

(b) **"Medical exemption"** means an exemption from one or more required immunizations granted on the basis of a licensed physician's certification that a specific vaccine is medically contraindicated or poses a significant health risk for an individual child.

(c) **"Licensed physician"** means a person holding a current, unrestricted license to practice medicine issued by the State Board of Medical Examiners, holding the degree of Doctor of Medicine (M.D.) or Doctor of Osteopathic Medicine (D.O.).

(d) **"School"** means any public or private institution providing instruction in grades pre-kindergarten through twelve, including charter schools and virtual academies requiring any in-person attendance.

(e) **"Childcare facility"** means any licensed or registered facility providing care, supervision, or instruction to children under six years of age outside the child's home.

## Section 3: Elimination of Non-Medical Exemptions

(a) No exemption from the immunization requirements of this State based upon personal, philosophical, moral, or religious beliefs shall be recognized for purposes of enrollment in any school or childcare facility.

(b) The sole exemption from immunization requirements for school or childcare enrollment shall be a medical exemption issued in accordance with Section 4 of this Act.

(c) Any existing statute, regulation, or administrative rule permitting non-medical exemptions from school or childcare immunization requirements is hereby superseded to the extent of any conflict with this Act.

## Section 4: Medical Exemption Standards

(a) A medical exemption shall be valid only if certified by a licensed physician on a form prescribed by the State Department of Health.

(b) The certification shall specify:

(1) The specific vaccine or vaccines for which the exemption is sought;

(2) The medical condition or conditions that contraindicate administration of each such vaccine; and

(3) Whether the exemption is temporary or permanent in nature, and, if temporary, the expected duration.

(c) All medical exemptions shall be subject to annual renewal. The licensed physician must recertify the continuing medical necessity of the exemption each year prior to the start of the school term.

(d) The State Department of Health shall review medical exemptions and may request additional documentation or refer cases for independent medical review where the rate of exemptions issued by a single physician exceeds a threshold established by departmental rule.

## Section 5: Conditional Enrollment

(a) A child who has not received all required immunizations but who is not seeking an exemption may enroll conditionally in a school or childcare facility, provided the child:

(1) Has received at least the first dose of each required vaccine series; and

(2) Is on an active catch-up schedule to complete all remaining doses in accordance with the immunization timeline recommended by the State Department of Health.

(b) Conditional enrollment shall be reviewed at intervals not to exceed ninety (90) days. A child who fails to remain current with the approved catch-up schedule shall be excluded from attendance until compliance is restored.

## Section 6: Public Education and Outreach

(a) The State Department of Health shall develop and fund a comprehensive public education program to inform parents, guardians, and healthcare providers about immunization requirements, the safety and efficacy of vaccines, and the provisions of this Act.

(b) The program shall include culturally and linguistically appropriate materials and shall prioritize outreach to communities with historically low vaccination rates.

(c) The State shall make available, at no cost to the family, all vaccines required under the immunization schedule for children who are uninsured or underinsured.

## Section 7: Severability

If any provision of this Act, or the application thereof to any person or circumstance, is held invalid by a court of competent jurisdiction, the invalidity shall not affect other provisions or applications of this Act that can be given effect without the invalid provision or application, and to this end the provisions of this Act are severable.

## Section 8: Effective Date

This Act shall take effect on the first day of the school year immediately following the date of enactment, provided that no fewer than one hundred and eighty (180) days have elapsed between the date of enactment and such first day. If fewer than one hundred and eighty days remain before the start of the next school year, this Act shall take effect on the first day of the subsequent school year.

## Policy Considerations

This model legislation represents one approach to standardizing immunization exemption policies for school and childcare entry. Legislators considering this template should evaluate:

- Constitutional and legal challenges related to the elimination of religious exemptions, including First Amendment free exercise claims and state Religious Freedom Restoration Act statutes that may apply in some jurisdictions
- The impact on families who hold sincere religious or philosophical objections, and whether alternative accommodations such as homeschool provisions adequately address their concerns
- Whether the medical exemption review process imposes undue burdens on physicians or creates barriers for children with legitimate but uncommon contraindications
- Fiscal impact on state budgets and healthcare systems
- Compatibility with existing state statutes and regulatory frameworks

The SAFE Research Institute presents this template as an educational resource and does not advocate for or against any specific legislative outcome.

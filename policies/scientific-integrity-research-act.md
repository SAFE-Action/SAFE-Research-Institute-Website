# Scientific Integrity in Public Health Research Act

**AN ACT** relating to scientific integrity; establishing protections for state-funded public health researchers; prohibiting political interference with scientific research and findings; mandating transparent research methodology; providing whistleblower protections; and providing for related matters.

*Be it enacted by the Legislature of the State of [STATE]:*

## Section 1. Purpose and Findings

(a) The Legislature finds and declares that:

1. Sound public health policy depends upon the free, transparent, and objective conduct of scientific research, unimpeded by political considerations or ideological pressure.
2. In recent years, documented instances of political interference with federal public health agencies, including the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and the National Institutes of Health, have undermined public confidence in scientific institutions and compromised the integrity of public health guidance.
3. Researchers employed by or funded through state agencies have reported instances of pressure to alter findings, delay publication of results, or refrain from investigating topics deemed politically sensitive, to the detriment of public health.
4. The suppression, alteration, or selective release of scientific findings for political purposes poses a direct threat to the health and safety of the public, as policymakers and health care providers rely on accurate and timely scientific evidence to inform decisions affecting millions of residents.
5. Multiple federal scientific integrity policies established between 2009 and 2022 demonstrated the feasibility and importance of institutional safeguards for research independence, and changes to federal scientific integrity frameworks have prompted interest in state-level approaches to research governance.
6. It is in the interest of the state to establish robust, enforceable protections for scientific integrity in public health research conducted with state funds or under state authority.

(b) The purpose of this Act is to safeguard the integrity, independence, and transparency of public health research conducted by or on behalf of the state, to protect researchers from retaliation for adhering to scientific standards, and to ensure that the public and policymakers have access to accurate, timely, and complete scientific information.

## Section 2. Definitions

As used in this Act:

1. **"Covered agency"** means any state agency, department, board, commission, or public institution of higher education that conducts, funds, or oversees public health research, including but not limited to the state department of health, the state environmental agency, and public universities.
2. **"Covered researcher"** means any individual who conducts public health research in whole or in part with state funds, under state authority, or as an employee or contractor of a covered agency, including principal investigators, co-investigators, research staff, laboratory personnel, and graduate research assistants.
3. **"Department"** means the state department of health or its successor agency.
4. **"Inspector General"** means the state inspector general, the inspector general of the relevant covered agency, or a designated independent entity as established under Section 7 of this Act.
5. **"Political interference"** means any action by an elected official, political appointee, or other non-scientific personnel that is intended to, or has the effect of, improperly influencing the design, conduct, analysis, reporting, or dissemination of public health research for reasons unrelated to scientific merit, methodological rigor, or legal compliance.
6. **"Public health research"** means systematic investigation, including research development, testing, evaluation, and dissemination of findings, designed to develop or contribute to generalizable knowledge related to the prevention of disease, promotion of health, or understanding of environmental, behavioral, or biological determinants of health.
7. **"Retaliation"** means any adverse employment action, including termination, demotion, suspension, reassignment, reduction in funding, denial of promotion, or other materially adverse change in the terms or conditions of employment or funding, taken against a covered researcher in response to protected activity under this Act.
8. **"Scientific findings"** means the data, analyses, conclusions, and recommendations resulting from public health research.
9. **"Scientific Integrity Officer"** means the individual designated under Section 3 of this Act.

## Section 3. Scientific Integrity Officer

(a) Each covered agency shall designate a Scientific Integrity Officer who shall:

1. Be a senior-level employee with demonstrated expertise in scientific research methodology and ethics;
2. Report directly to the head of the covered agency on matters relating to scientific integrity;
3. Not be subject to removal except for cause, following notice and an opportunity to be heard;
4. Not hold any concurrent political appointment or serve at the pleasure of any elected official; and
5. Serve a fixed term of not less than four years, which may be renewed.

(b) The Scientific Integrity Officer shall have the authority and responsibility to:

1. Develop, implement, and enforce a scientific integrity policy for the covered agency consistent with the requirements of this Act;
2. Receive and investigate complaints of political interference or violations of scientific integrity;
3. Issue findings and recommendations to the agency head, which shall be made public within thirty days of issuance;
4. Publish an annual report on the state of scientific integrity within the covered agency, including the number and disposition of complaints received; and
5. Coordinate with the Inspector General on matters involving potential violations of law.

## Section 4. Protections for Research Independence

(a) No elected official, political appointee, or other non-scientific personnel shall:

1. Direct or coerce a covered researcher to alter, suppress, delay, or misrepresent scientific findings;
2. Prohibit or restrict a covered researcher from publishing, presenting, or otherwise disseminating scientific findings in peer-reviewed journals, at professional conferences, or through other customary channels of scientific communication;
3. Require pre-publication review of scientific manuscripts by political appointees or non-scientific communications staff for purposes other than ensuring compliance with applicable law regarding classified information, privacy, or intellectual property;
4. Remove or alter scientific content from agency websites, publications, or public communications without the written concurrence of the principal investigator or the Scientific Integrity Officer;
5. Prohibit the use of specific scientific terms, methodologies, or analytical frameworks in research documents or public communications for non-scientific reasons;
6. Terminate, reduce, or redirect research funding based on the anticipated or actual conclusions of the research rather than on scientific merit, programmatic relevance, or fiscal necessity; or
7. Interfere with the selection, appointment, or tenure of members of scientific advisory committees on the basis of the political implications of their scientific views.

(b) Nothing in this section shall be construed to:

1. Prevent the exercise of legitimate supervisory authority over research conducted by covered agencies, including decisions related to research priorities, resource allocation based on programmatic needs, or compliance with applicable law;
2. Restrict the authority of covered agencies to establish policies governing conflicts of interest, human subjects protection, data security, or other matters unrelated to the suppression of scientific findings; or
3. Prohibit an agency head from requiring factual accuracy review of scientific communications by qualified scientific personnel prior to public release.

## Section 5. Transparent Research Methodology

(a) Each covered agency shall adopt and maintain policies ensuring that:

1. All public health research funded by or conducted under the authority of the agency adheres to established standards of scientific methodology, including peer review, reproducibility, and transparent reporting of methods, data, and analytical techniques;
2. Research protocols, data sets, and analytical methods are made publicly available to the extent permitted by law, with appropriate protections for personally identifiable information, proprietary information, and ongoing investigations;
3. Research findings are published in a timely manner, and no scientific finding shall be withheld from publication for longer than twelve months from the completion of the research without a written justification approved by the Scientific Integrity Officer;
4. All public health research reports published by the agency include a clear description of the methodology, funding sources, potential conflicts of interest, and any limitations of the research; and
5. Covered researchers are permitted and encouraged to include their own professional interpretations and policy implications in scientific publications, clearly distinguished from official agency positions where appropriate.

(b) The department shall establish and maintain a publicly accessible online repository of all public health research reports produced by covered agencies, including metadata sufficient to identify the research topic, investigators, funding source, and date of completion.

## Section 6. Whistleblower Protections

(a) No covered agency or officer thereof shall take or threaten to take any retaliatory action against a covered researcher who, in good faith:

1. Reports a suspected violation of this Act to the Scientific Integrity Officer, the Inspector General, a member of the Legislature, or any other appropriate authority;
2. Refuses to engage in conduct that would constitute a violation of this Act;
3. Provides testimony or cooperates with an investigation under this Act; or
4. Exercises any right protected under this Act.

(b) A covered researcher who is subjected to retaliation in violation of this section may:

1. File a complaint with the Scientific Integrity Officer or the Inspector General within one hundred eighty days of the retaliatory action;
2. Bring a civil action in a court of competent jurisdiction within two years of the retaliatory action; and
3. Obtain relief including:
   - (i) Reinstatement to the position held prior to the retaliation, or to an equivalent position;
   - (ii) Back pay with interest, and restoration of any lost benefits or seniority;
   - (iii) Compensatory damages for harm suffered as a result of the retaliation;
   - (iv) Reasonable attorneys' fees and costs of litigation; and
   - (v) Such other equitable relief as the court deems necessary to make the covered researcher whole.

(c) In any proceeding under this section, a rebuttable presumption of retaliation shall arise if an adverse employment action is taken within twelve months of the covered researcher engaging in protected activity under this Act. The burden shall then shift to the covered agency to demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that the adverse action was taken for legitimate, non-retaliatory reasons.

## Section 7. Enforcement

(a) The Inspector General shall have the authority to investigate complaints of violations of this Act, including political interference and retaliation, and to issue findings and recommendations.

(b) A covered agency that is found to have violated this Act may be subject to:

1. A requirement to restore any suppressed, altered, or withdrawn scientific findings to their original form and make them publicly available;
2. Mandatory corrective action, including revision of policies and training of personnel;
3. Reporting to the Legislature and the Governor on the violation and corrective actions taken; and
4. Such other remedies as may be appropriate under applicable law.

(c) An individual who knowingly and willfully directs or coerces a covered researcher to violate this Act, or who knowingly and willfully retaliates against a covered researcher in violation of Section 6, shall be subject to:

1. Disciplinary action, up to and including termination of employment;
2. Civil penalties of not less than five thousand dollars ($5,000) nor more than fifty thousand dollars ($50,000) per violation; and
3. Referral to the appropriate law enforcement authority if the conduct constitutes a criminal offense under other applicable law.

## Section 8. Scientific Integrity Advisory Board

(a) There is hereby established a Scientific Integrity Advisory Board, composed of not fewer than seven members appointed as follows:

1. Three members appointed by the Governor, each of whom shall be a scientist with expertise in public health, epidemiology, or biomedical research and shall not hold any political office or appointment;
2. Two members appointed by the Legislature, one by the presiding officer of each chamber, each of whom shall be a scientist or ethicist with expertise relevant to scientific integrity;
3. One member appointed by the state's public university system, who shall be a tenured faculty member with an active research program in public health or a related discipline; and
4. One member who is a covered researcher, elected by covered researchers across the state's covered agencies.

(b) The Board shall:

1. Review and provide recommendations on the scientific integrity policies of covered agencies;
2. Hear appeals from covered researchers who are dissatisfied with the disposition of complaints by a Scientific Integrity Officer;
3. Publish a biennial report assessing the state of scientific integrity in public health research across the state; and
4. Advise the Legislature on any legislative changes needed to strengthen protections for scientific integrity.

## Section 9. Periodic Review and Reporting

(a) Each covered agency shall conduct an internal review of its compliance with this Act not less than once every two years and shall submit a report of its findings to the Scientific Integrity Advisory Board and the relevant committees of the Legislature.

(b) The Scientific Integrity Advisory Board shall compile the agency reports and publish a comprehensive statewide assessment of scientific integrity, which shall be made available to the public on the department's website.

## Section 10. Severability

If any provision of this Act, or its application to any person or circumstance, is held invalid, the invalidity does not affect other provisions or applications of this Act that can be given effect without the invalid provision or application, and to this end the provisions of this Act are severable.

## Section 11. Effective Date

This Act shall take effect on [DATE], or upon passage and approval, whichever is later.

## Policy Considerations

This model legislation represents one approach to establishing institutional protections for scientific research independence at the state level. Legislators considering this template should evaluate:

- The balance between protecting researcher independence and preserving legitimate executive authority over agency priorities, resource allocation, and public communications
- Whether whistleblower protections and civil penalty provisions may create unintended consequences, such as chilling legitimate supervisory feedback or encouraging frivolous complaints
- Administrative costs of establishing and staffing Scientific Integrity Officers, advisory boards, and complaint investigation processes across multiple covered agencies
- Fiscal impact on state budgets and healthcare systems
- Compatibility with existing state statutes and regulatory frameworks

The SAFE Research Institute presents this template as an educational resource and does not advocate for or against any specific legislative outcome.

Science Communication

Become a Science Communicator

Empowering physicians, scientists, and public health professionals to bridge the critical gap between peer-reviewed research and the policymakers, institutions, and communities that depend on it.

Your Roadmap

How to Become a Science Communicator

Effective science communication is a skill built through deliberate practice and cross-disciplinary engagement. Follow these foundational steps to establish yourself as a trusted voice.

Step 1

Build Your Foundation

Invest in formal education in your scientific discipline and stay current with the literature. A deep understanding of research methodology, statistics, and peer review is essential before you can effectively translate findings for non-specialist audiences.

Step 2

Develop Communication Skills

Practice distilling complex concepts into clear, accurate language. Take courses in science writing, public speaking, and media training. Learn to adjust your register for different audiences without sacrificing scientific integrity or precision.

Step 3

Find Your Platform

Identify the medium that best suits your strengths and your target audience. Options include policy briefs, journal commentary, institutional blogs, public testimony, podcast appearances, video explainers, and presentations to legislative committees.

Step 4

Build Credibility

Publish consistently, cite your sources transparently, and acknowledge the limits of available data. Credibility is earned through accuracy, intellectual honesty, and a willingness to correct the record when new evidence emerges.

Step 5

Connect with Organizations

Partner with research institutes, professional associations, and public health organizations. Institutional affiliation lends authority to your work and connects you with networks of peers, policymakers, and media professionals who amplify evidence-based messaging.

Step 6

Stay Current & Evolve

Science communication is an evolving discipline. Monitor new research in your field, study how public understanding shifts over time, and refine your techniques. Attend workshops, seek feedback from diverse audiences, and never stop learning.

Policy Engagement

Working with Legislators

Presenting research to lawmakers requires a different approach than writing for academic journals. Legislators operate under time constraints, competing priorities, and constituent pressures. These best practices will help you deliver scientific evidence in a way that supports informed decision-making.

Lead with the Problem, Not the Data

Open with the policy challenge or public health concern. Frame data as the tool for understanding the problem, not the starting point of the conversation.

Use Plain Language, Not Jargon

Replace technical terminology with clear, everyday language. If a term is unavoidable, define it immediately. Your goal is comprehension, not demonstration of expertise.

Provide One-Page Policy Briefs

Condense your findings into a single-page summary with key takeaways, supporting data points, and recommended actions. Legislators and their staff need material they can absorb quickly.

MORE BEST PRACTICES
Offer to Be a Resource, Not an Advocate
Position yourself as a neutral expert available to answer questions. Legislators are more receptive to information sources that do not push a specific outcome.
Respect the Legislative Process
Understand committee structures, hearing schedules, and the legislative calendar. Timing your outreach to align with active deliberations greatly increases impact.
Follow Up Consistently
Building relationships with legislative offices takes sustained effort. Provide updated data when available, respond promptly to inquiries, and maintain professionalism at every stage.
Public Engagement

Communicating Research to the Public

Translating complex science into accessible content is one of the most important responsibilities of a science communicator. These principles will help you reach broader audiences without compromising accuracy.

Principle

Know Your Audience

Research who you are speaking to before crafting your message. A community health presentation, a newspaper op-ed, and a social media post each require different approaches to tone, depth, and format.

Principle

Use Analogies and Stories

Narrative is one of the most powerful tools for making data memorable. Ground abstract findings in relatable scenarios, everyday comparisons, and real-world examples that connect with your audience's lived experience.

Principle

Address Misconceptions Directly

Do not shy away from common myths or misunderstandings. Name them explicitly, explain why they persist, and present the corrective evidence clearly. Ignoring misinformation allows it to fill the void left by silence.

Principle

Cite Your Sources Transparently

Always reference the peer-reviewed studies, datasets, or institutional reports that underpin your claims. Transparency about where your information comes from builds trust and empowers your audience to verify your assertions independently.

Principle

Leverage Multiple Media Formats

Different audiences consume information in different ways. Combine written briefs, infographics, video explainers, podcasts, and interactive data visualizations to maximize reach and engagement across demographics.
